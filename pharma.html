The Industry

Computation is an incredible asset to the pharmaceutical industry. The progress of pharmaceuticals has accelerated drastically with the introduction of computers, allowing scientists to focus their efforts on innovation on developing new medinices, while the slow, repetitive, and necessary work happens in the background.
A less medically-focused example is budgeting software. It allows labs to optimise their cash flow (which they often have a lot of), helping them invest more into research instead of spending time sorting their finances out. Another example of computers being incredibly useful is the supply chain, allowing rapid requests and deliveries of critical equipment and reagents.
But these aren't even directly related to medicine - read on to find the top 3 computation benefits in pharma!

Simulation

"Biological systems are so inherently complex that only efficient models running on state of the art hardware can begin to simulate reality."


-J. A. Schmidt et al, 2010

In the old days, we tested everything that was believed to be a medicine. As you might expect, most of these things weren't medicines. We progressed onto testing on living creatures similar biologically to humans, like monkeys. We know how people responded to that. Although we still trial with animals and rodents, a new contender has emerged: the computer.
Advanced simulations and models enable rapid testing of potential drugs, and their influence, on smaller closed systems. This is not yet anywhere near the accuracy of testing on live subjects, but it can often indicate the approximate results and timescales.

Solving

"...has been an important open research problem for more than 50 years."


-John Jumper et al, 2021

Not unlike the applications of computers in solving mathematical or technological problems, they can also be used to solve biological problems. A prime example of this is Google Deepmind's AlphaFold project in 2021. It applied cutting-edge artificial intelligence technology to solve problems, at a rate of orders of magnitude faster than it's predecessors. The problem in question was protein folding - predicting protein structures based on a protein sequence. This has tremendous applications in bio-science, and unlocks the door for medical companies to investigate this branch of science much more readily.
A similar project that you have likely heard of was Fold@Home, a distributed computation solution to achieve the same thing, also using computers.

Sales

"...the recent focus on AI and ML is changing the picture rapidly."


Rama K. Malladi

The field of finance and economy is dominated by mathematics, the kind of field that computation is designed to process. It leads to, no surprise, mathematical models of the market. Big Pharmaceutical can apply such models to maximise their sales and offerings in ways that would otherwise be difficult and wildly inconsistent. These models, often applying Machine Learning to predict scenarios, will map out the spread, distribution, and demand of various types of drugs and treatments. This allows corporate to maximise profits more easily, which in turn creates more budget for the scientists who develop medicine. More budget means more science, and it's all possible thanks to computation!
