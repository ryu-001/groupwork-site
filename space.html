Spaceflight and Computing

Computing has a long history in spaceflight, from the first artificial satellite to orbit the Earth, to the very latest in spaceflight technology, being used both in space and on the ground.

Sputnik I

The Soviet Union launched Sputnik I in 1957, kicking off a new era in spaceflight.
While the equipment on board was very simple, only capable of sending a simple repetitive pulse, the satellite contained a temperature regulation system and pressure sensors. If the spacecraft's internal pressure or temperature passed certain thresholds, the duration of the pulses would change to indicate the seals or the thermal control system had failed. The way the recieved pulses travelled was also used to make deductions about the Earth's ionosphere. Computers were also used in the planning for the Sputnik I mission, calculating the parareters of its orbit.

Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS)

A GNSS is a system that uses Earth-orbiting satellites emitting highly accurate timing information over radio frequencies. These can be recieved by observers on Earth and by measuring the relative timing of signals many satellites, the difference in distance to each satellite can be determined and from this the reciever's position can be triangulated, without the need for the reciever to emit any signals. This is convenient for navigation as the reciever can be low-power and low-profile while still being able to determine its position, anywhere on Earth, with high accuracy.
Many nations have constructed or are constructing their own GNSS constellations, including:

  • USA - GPS (Global Positioning System) - 31 satellites
  • Russia - GLONASS (Globalnaya Navigazionnaya Sputnikovaya Sistema) - 24+ satellites
  • China - BDS (BeiDou Navigation Satellite System) - 35 satellites
  • EU - Galileo - 24 satellites
  • India - NavIC (Navigation Indian Constellation) - regional only - 7 satellites
  • Japan - QZSS (Quasi-Zenith Satellite System) - regional only - 4 satellites

Apollo Guidance Computer

The Apollo Guidance Computer (AGC) was a computer designed in the early 1960s to be installed on the Apollo Command Module and Lunar Module spacecraft, used in the first — and so far only — human landings on the Moon.
This computer was remarkable in many aspects - it was the first computer to be built with silicon integrated circuits.
The AGC had a 16-bit word size, with 1 bit being a parity bit to detect when memory corruption occurs and show a warning light.
It used a 2048KHz clock, executing an instruction every 2 cycles of this clock, at roughly 1MHz. It had 1000 words (equivalent to ~2KB) of memory in each of its memory banks. Bank 0 was modifiable RAM, and all subsequent banks were read-only. The version used in manned flights had 36 banks of fixed memory, giving the AGC 36 kilowords, approximately 72 kilobytes of fixed memory for storing programs and constant data.
